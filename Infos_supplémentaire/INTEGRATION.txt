
Catalog Integration
Common foundation for all teams. Read README.md first for the hackathon context.
1. Your mission
Design a solution that helps the Nordika Studio team integrate their catalog on 6 marketplaces with minimal manual effort. Your solution must handle messy data, translate content, map categories to each marketplace's taxonomy, validate the output, and give the seller clear control over what gets published.

The form of the solution is up to you — a web app, a conversational agent, a command-line tool with a rich feedback loop, a custom-trained model, a workflow of specialized agents, or anything in between. What matters is that a non-technical seller can understand, trust, and control what the system does.
What the agent must do
Read the Nordika source catalog (CSV).
Normalize inconsistent values if needed (casing, typos, boolean variants, separators), if necessary
Recover missing attributes by extracting information from titles, descriptions and specifications, if necessary
Translate titles, descriptions and localized attribute values into IT and DE.
Map each Nordika category to an Amazon product_type (same for all countries) and a Google Product Category (specific to each country).
Validate each product against the target schema (required fields, constraints, enums).
Produce 6 compliant marketplace feeds.
What the seller must be able to do
A black-box pipeline that silently produces 6 files is not enough. Whatever form your solution takes, the seller must be able to trigger, monitor, inspect, override, and learn from the integration process. Think about: transparency of decisions, handling of edge cases, bulk vs per-SKU control, human-in-the-loop for ambiguous cases, progress feedback, error recovery.
Seller pain points to solve
Every new product has to be integrated on 6 marketplaces, each with its own rules.
Titles and descriptions must be translated into Italian and German.
The internal catalog is noisy: missing values, typos, inconsistent casing.
Marketplace validation errors mean back-and-forth edits that cost hours per SKU.

3. Output format — Amazon
Format: JSONL (one product per line, UTF-8)
Filename pattern: amazon_{country}_feed.jsonl (e.g., amazon_fr_feed.jsonl)

The machine-readable schema is split across schemas/amazon_common_attributes.yaml (attributes shared by all product_types) and schemas/amazon_product_type_attributes.yaml (specific_attributes per product_type) — use them if your agent needs to parse or validate attribute rules programmatically. The sections below summarize the key information for quick reading.
Locale handling
Each feed targets a single marketplace. The marketplace_id field on every line determines the locale for all attributes flagged localized: true in the YAML references. Mapping in schemas/amazon_common_attributes.yaml &gt; feed_metadata &gt; marketplace_id &gt; values.

Attribute resolution
For each SKU:

Take all common_attributes with their default requirement level.
Add all specific_attributes of the chosen product_type.

A feed line is valid if every required attribute (common + specific) is present and satisfies its constraints.
Salient constraints
item_name: 1 to 200 characters, no ALL CAPS, no promotional characters (!, ★).
bullet_points: 3 to 5 bullets, each 1 to 250 characters, sentence case.
product_description: 1 to 2000 characters, HTML forbidden.
Localized attributes: item_name, product_description, bullet_points, color, material.

4. Output format — Google Shopping
Format: TSV (tab-separated values, UTF-8)
Filename pattern: google_{country}_feed.tsv

The machine-readable schema is in schemas/google_shopping_attributes.yaml — use it if your agent needs to parse or validate attribute rules programmatically. The tables below summarize the same information for quick reading.
Locale handling
Each feed targets a single locale declared in feed_metadata.feed_locale (fr_FR, it_IT, de_DE). All attributes flagged localized: true in the YAML must be expressed in that language — including title, description, color, material, product_type, and the localized form of google_product_category.

Constraints
title and description must be in the target-country language.
color and material must be localized (e.g., Chêne for FR, Rovere for IT, Eiche for DE).
price must use the format &lt;amount&gt; EUR (e.g., 302.00 EUR), same price across all locales.
TSV encoding: no raw tabs inside values, line breaks escaped as literal \n, multi-value fields (e.g. product_highlight) use | as separator.

5. Business rules
Stock
Each product has an initial stock quantity in the stock_quantity column of the source catalog. Use it as-is for the initial integration.

For Amazon, populate fulfillment_availability[0].quantity with stock_quantity.
For Google, derive availability: in_stock if stock_quantity &gt; 0, otherwise out_of_stock.
A small number of SKUs will have stock_quantity = 0 — your agent must decide whether to publish them anyway (as out_of_stock) or skip them entirely. Both are defensible; document the choice.

Stock management across marketplaces (allocation, restocking, sync) is the subject of the Order processing &amp; supply deep dive.
Pricing
Each product has a single price in the price_eur column — this is the final selling price in EUR, the same on all marketplaces, for all countries. Publish it as-is. No margin computation, no VAT handling, no currency conversion to manage here.

Price optimization is the subject of the Dynamic pricing deep dive.
Translation
For FR markets: use title_fr and description_fr as-is.
For IT and DE markets: translate from title_en and description_en. English is the safer pivot because the French text sometimes contains encoding artifacts and typos.
For localized attribute values: an attribute flagged localized: true must also be translated (e.g., color Cream becomes Crème in FR, Crema in IT, Creme in DE).
Required is a minimum, not a target ⚠️
Passing validation with only the required fields filled is not a success. Marketplaces rank and filter products based on how complete their listings are:

On Amazon, listings with rich attributes (material, color, style, max_weight_recommendation, etc.) appear in more filter facets and rank higher in relevance. A product with only the bare minimum is often invisible in category browse pages.
On Google Shopping, the recommended columns (color, material) directly affect ad eligibility and CTR. Products missing them show up less in shopping ads.
Bullet points and detailed descriptions drive organic conversions. &quot;Valid but empty&quot; listings convert poorly.

Rule of thumb for your agent: fill every attribute you can reasonably derive from the source data — from the structured columns, from free text extraction, or from sensible defaults. Required fields are the floor; completeness is what sells.

Treat these files as a checkpoint, not a final deliverable. Getting them right means your mapping, translation, normalization and validation logic all work end-to-end. Getting them wrong (missing fields, wrong language, broken enums) tells you exactly where your pipeline breaks.

Tip: writing a small auto-check that parses your feeds and verifies them against the YAML schemas (required fields present, enums valid, lengths within bounds, locale correct) is a cheap way to catch regressions while you iterate. Bonus if the seller can trigger it from your solution and get a pass/fail report per marketplace.

7. Good to know
The full attribute requirements depend on the chosen product_type. Picking FURNITURE as a catch-all is technically valid but makes your listings poor. Picking a too-specific type may force requirements you cannot satisfy.
Bullet points don't exist in the source catalog. You must generate them from description_* and specifications.
Bonus — watch out for scale: a naive &quot;one LLM call per SKU per marketplace&quot; means 1,200 calls just for 200 products. Think about caching, batching, and which parts actually need an LLM vs simple rules.
